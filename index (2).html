Synapse Match

Computer Science • Emerald High School • Fall 2025

Synapse Match is an AI application that detects early-stage indicators of Parkinson's disease. Inspired by our physics teacher's unexpected diagnosis, our team of four juniors built a system that analyzes three input types simultaneously: a user survey, an audio recording for vocal analysis, and a motor assessment. These signals feed into a prediction pipeline that estimates the likelihood of Parkinson's onset. The app won first place in California's 10th Congressional District, recognized by Congressman Mark DeSaulnier.

My Responsibilities

I trained the machine learning model, built the core app, and designed the full data processing and prediction pipeline from raw inputs through to the final risk output.

What I Learned

Working under a real competition deadline with meaningful stakes taught me to make fast, confident engineering decisions. I learned how to preprocess noisy multimodal data, handle the tradeoffs between model accuracy and interpretability in a healthcare context, and build something people can actually trust. The human-centered problem motivated every technical choice we made.

Bias in Multi-Agent AI Systems

Independent Research • 2024–2025

Prior research had studied implicit bias in single AI systems, but almost no work had looked at how bias behaves when AI models interact with each other. I identified this gap and designed a research study to explore it. The core question: does bias get amplified when agents interact? To test this, I introduced task-based scenarios and a multi-agent judging framework that had not been used before in this context. The paper was accepted to both ICLR and NAACL, two of the most selective venues in the AI and NLP research community.

My Responsibilities

I came up with the original research idea, led the experiment design and methodology, and was the primary contributor to the multi-agent judging framework. I contributed significantly to the paper write-up and analysis of results.

What I Learned

This project taught me how to go from a vague intuition ("do multi-agent systems amplify bias?") to a rigorous, peer-reviewed methodology. I learned how to conduct a literature review at a real research level, how to design experiments that isolate variables cleanly, and how to write about findings precisely enough to satisfy reviewers at top-tier academic conferences.
